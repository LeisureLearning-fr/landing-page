The best tools to revise for the NEBOSH IGC in 2026

By Attila Young, HSE Consultant · Updated 19 July 2026 · 9 min read

There's no single "best" NEBOSH IGC revision tool — it depends on where you are in your prep, and whether the resource has actually been updated for the 2026 GIC1/GIC2 syllabus. This guide breaks down the main types of revision tools candidates use, what each one is genuinely good for, and the mistake that costs the most marks: revising with material built for the old, retired specification.

Why the 2026 syllabus change matters for your revision tool

NEBOSH refreshed the International General Certificate for 2026, restructuring the qualification into two units — GIC1 (managing health and safety) and GIC2 (risk assessment) — with teaching starting from February 2026 and the first assessments sitting from March 2026. GIC2 also moved to a points-based marking approach, which changes how much detail an answer needs to actually earn credit.

Practically, that means a large share of the flashcard decks, PDF question banks, and forum threads circulating online were written for the retired IG1/IG2 specification. The terminology has shifted, some legal references have moved, and the GIC1 exam format is different from what older resources describe. None of that makes those resources bad — it just means a lot of candidates are unknowingly revising for an exam that no longer exists in that form.

The first thing worth checking on any tool, book, or question bank before you rely on it: does it say GIC1/GIC2 2026, or is it still built around IG1/IG2?

What actually makes a revision tool worth using

Past the syllabus-year check, a handful of things separate a revision tool that moves the needle from one that just feels productive:

Alignment

Built for GIC1/GIC2 2026 specifically

Not a relabelled IG1/IG2 bank. Content, terminology, and legal references should match the current syllabus.

Feedback

Marks written and scenario answers, not just MCQ

Most GIC1 and GIC2 marks come from open and scenario answers. A tool that only does multiple choice skips most of the exam.

Exam format

Practises the real GIC1 scenario-and-attachments format

GIC1 is open-book with supporting documents. Practice that ignores this format leaves the biggest surprise for exam day.

Visibility

Tracks your weak points by syllabus element

So you can tell, element by element, whether you're ready to book — instead of guessing from a gut feeling.

The main types of NEBOSH IGC revision tools, compared

Here's how the five main categories candidates actually use stack up.

1. Flashcard apps (Quizlet, Brainscape, Cram)

Free – low cost

Good for: drilling definitions, legislation names, and key terms in short bursts between other work.

Watch out for: most public decks predate the 2026 syllabus and were built around IG1/IG2. They also don't give feedback on how you'd structure a written or scenario answer — where most of the marks actually sit.

2. PDF question-and-answer study guides

One-time purchase

Good for: a cheap reference on exam-style phrasing and structure, useful alongside other prep.

Watch out for: static content that can't adapt to what you personally struggle with, no marking of your own written answers, and inconsistent quality — check the syllabus year before buying.

3. Full training-provider platforms

Bundled with your course

Good for: this is where you should actually learn the content — structured teaching and tutor support, required to sit the exam.

Watch out for: the self-test element is usually a secondary feature bolted onto the learning platform, not the main focus. Buying a full course again just for more practice questions is expensive.

4. Shared exam papers & forum question banks

Free

Good for: getting a feel for real exam phrasing and past question style.

Watch out for: no quality control on model answers, mixed syllabus versions in the same thread, and the risk of memorising an outdated or simply incorrect answer.

5. Adaptive, AI-marked practice tools

e.g. IGC Ready

Good for: practising the actual open-question and GIC1 scenario-and-attachments format, with detailed feedback on what you covered and what you missed, adaptive difficulty, and a progress tracker mapped to syllabus elements.

Watch out for: it's a revision and practice tool, not a substitute for the accredited training course you still need to complete before sitting the exam.

So, what should you actually use?

Honestly — a combination, used for different jobs:

  • For learning the content in the first place, your accredited course and its course book are still the foundation. No revision tool replaces that.
  • For quick recall of terms and legislation, a flashcard app costs nothing and works fine — just confirm the deck was updated for GIC1/GIC2 2026 before you trust it.
  • For the part that actually costs most candidates marks — writing answers examiners give credit for, and working through the open-book GIC1 scenario under exam-like conditions — you want a tool that grades your actual writing, not just multiple choice.

FAQ

What is the best way to revise for the NEBOSH IGC?

There isn't one single best tool — it depends on your stage of prep. Learn the content through an accredited course and its course book, use flashcards for quick recall of terms and legislation, and use a tool that grades your open and scenario answers, like IGC Ready, to practise exam technique for the current GIC1/GIC2 syllabus.

Can I use old NEBOSH IGC flashcards and question banks for the 2026 syllabus?

Some content still applies, but many public flashcard decks and PDF question banks were written for the retired IG1/IG2 specification. Check the syllabus year before relying on any free resource, since terminology, some legal references, and the GIC1 exam format have changed for 2026.

Is there an app to revise for NEBOSH IGC?

Yes. Options range from general flashcard apps like Quizlet and Brainscape to purpose-built NEBOSH IGC tools such as IGC Ready, which is rebuilt for the 2026 GIC1/GIC2 syllabus and grades open and scenario answers rather than only multiple choice.

How long should I spend revising before the NEBOSH IGC exam?

Most candidates spend several weeks revising after finishing their accredited course, though the right amount depends on how much time you can commit weekly and how confident you already are across all syllabus elements. A progress tracker mapped to syllabus elements is a more reliable way to judge readiness than picking a fixed number of weeks.

Do I still need a training course if I use a revision tool?

Yes. Revision tools and question banks are meant to be used after an accredited NEBOSH training course, to consolidate what you've learned and practise exam technique — they are not a replacement for the course itself.
