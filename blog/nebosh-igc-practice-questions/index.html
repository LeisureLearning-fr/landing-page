NEBOSH IGC Practice Questions: How to Actually Prepare for GIC1 and GIC2

Published Jul 20, 2026 · 8 min read

Short version: most free NEBOSH IGC practice question sets still drill isolated multiple-choice recall — written for the retired IG1/IG2 format. The current GIC1/GIC2 syllabus tests something different: reading a scenario, identifying what's actually going on in it, and writing a justified, specific answer. Practicing multiple-choice questions alone builds a skill the exam doesn't ask for. Here's why that gap exists, and how to practice for what GIC1/GIC2 actually tests instead.

Why multiple-choice question drilling doesn't transfer

The old IG1/IG2 format rewarded definition recall — five steps of risk assessment, the definition of a hazard, that kind of thing. GIC1 and GIC2 ask you to apply that knowledge inside a specific workplace scenario: identify what's actually wrong, assess the risk it creates, and propose controls that would genuinely work there.

Multiple-choice questions are a good first step — they train you to recognize the right answer. But GIC1 doesn't ask you to recognize; it asks you to write a paragraph that a human examiner has to be able to credit. That means expressing your reasoning from scratch, not selecting from options someone else wrote. Our tool picks up where multiple-choice leaves off: once you can spot the right answer, it pushes you to construct one yourself.

Open-book doesn't mean you can look everything up

It's tempting to treat open-book access as a safety net — "I'll just find it in the reference material on the day." The maths doesn't support that: GIC1 gives you a tight writing window once the tasks are in front of you, which doesn't leave room to read unfamiliar material from scratch. Open-book access is for verifying a detail or citing a regulation you already roughly know exists — not for learning the content during the exam itself. See Inside the NEBOSH GIC1 Exam for the full breakdown of what exam day actually looks like.

A short practice checklist

  • Don't rely on multiple-choice drilling alone — it doesn't build the writing skill GIC1/GIC2 actually test.
  • Practice against scenarios with supporting documents (method statements, incident reports), not standalone questions in isolation.
  • Write full justifications, not just the control measure — explain why it works for the scenario in front of you.
  • Apply hierarchy of control deliberately, in order, rather than jumping to PPE.
  • Time yourself realistically so exam-day pacing isn't a surprise.
  • Get feedback on why an answer works, not just whether it's right — that's the only way to stop repeating the same gap.

That last point is the one static question banks can't give you, no matter how current the questions are. If part of what's holding your prep back is practicing without knowing why an answer would or wouldn't work, that's the specific gap this tool is built to close.
