NEBOSH IGC Practice Questions: How to Actually Prepare for GIC1 and GIC2

Published Jul 20, 2026 · 8 min read

Short version: most free NEBOSH IGC practice question sets still drill isolated multiple-choice recall — written for the retired IG1/IG2 format. The current GIC1/GIC2 syllabus tests something different: reading a scenario, identifying what's actually going on in it, and writing a justified, specific answer. Practicing MCQs alone builds a skill the exam doesn't ask for. Here's what examiners are actually looking for in an open answer, and how to practice for that instead.

Why MCQ drilling doesn't transfer

The old IG1/IG2 format rewarded definition recall — five steps of risk assessment, the definition of a hazard, that kind of thing. GIC1 and GIC2 ask you to apply that knowledge inside a specific workplace scenario: identify what's actually wrong, assess the risk it creates, and propose controls that would genuinely work there. Those are different skills. A candidate who's only ever ticked boxes on a multiple-choice bank hasn't practiced the thing GIC1 actually tests — reading a scenario, then writing a paragraph that a human examiner has to be able to credit.

How examiners actually grade an open answer

The pattern that separates a middling answer from a strong one is consistent, and it comes down to specificity and justification rather than length.

  • Vague vs. specific control measures. "Provide more training on safety" earns little. "Induction training covering manual handling risk for pallets over 20kg, with quarterly refresher sessions" earns credit — because it shows you understood the actual hazard, not just that training exists as a concept.
  • Stating vs. justifying. "Use a harness for work at height" states a control. "Use a harness because a fall from this platform risks serious injury, and a harness prevents an uncontrolled fall" justifies it — and the justification is usually where the marks sit.
  • Hierarchy of control, applied. Jumping straight to PPE ("tell workers to be careful") without considering elimination, substitution or engineering controls first reads as a gap in understanding, not just a missed option.
Key takeaway: an answer that references specific details from the scenario in front of you will almost always outscore a technically-correct answer written from memory. Generic is the thing that costs marks, not being wrong.

Open-book doesn't mean you can look everything up

It's tempting to treat open-book access as a safety net — "I'll just find it in the reference material on the day." The maths doesn't support that: GIC1 gives you roughly 2 to 3 minutes per mark once you're actually writing, which doesn't leave room to read unfamiliar material from scratch. Open-book access is for verifying a detail or citing a regulation you already roughly know exists — not for learning the content during the exam itself. A structure that makes your answer easy to mark

Examiners read a lot of answers. A simple point → evidence → explanation structure makes yours faster to credit: state the issue, back it with a fact or the relevant regulation, then explain why it matters for the specific scenario in front of you — not H&S in general. That last step, tying it back to the scenario's actual details, is usually what's missing from an answer that's technically accurate but still underperforms.

A short practice checklist

  • Don't rely on multiple-choice drilling alone — it doesn't build the writing skill GIC1/GIC2 actually test.
  • Practice against scenarios with supporting documents (method statements, incident reports), not standalone questions in isolation.
  • Write full justifications, not just the control measure — "why" is usually where the marks are.
  • Apply hierarchy of control deliberately, in order, rather than jumping to PPE.
  • Time yourself against a real per-mark budget so exam-day pacing isn't a surprise.
  • Get feedback on why an answer scored what it did, not just whether it was right — that's the only way to stop repeating the same gap.

That last point is the one static question banks can't give you, no matter how current the questions are. If part of what's holding your prep back is practicing without knowing why an answer would or wouldn't score well, that's the specific gap this tool is built to close.
