# LTEL and STEL: Two Numbers, Two Very Different Jobs

*Meta description: The difference between LTEL and STEL exposure limits explained — which health effect each one is actually designed to guard against.*
*Suggested keywords: LTEL vs STEL, occupational exposure limits explained, NEBOSH OEL*
*Angle: exam-tips*

Two acronyms, two time periods, two completely different jobs — and yet candidates regularly use them interchangeably, which is exactly what turns an easy mark into a lost one.

## What an exposure limit actually is

An occupational exposure limit sets the maximum airborne concentration of a hazardous substance, averaged over a defined period, that a worker shouldn't be exposed above. It's a legal ceiling, not a target to aim for — exposure should still be reduced as low as reasonably practicable below the limit, not treated as a licence to hit it exactly.

## LTEL: the long game

The **long-term exposure limit** is averaged over roughly an 8-hour period, and it exists to guard against **chronic** effects — the slow, cumulative damage that builds up from repeated exposure over months or years. Think conditions that develop gradually: certain lung diseases, long-term organ damage, the kind of harm you wouldn't notice after a single shift but absolutely would after years of it.

## STEL: the spike

The **short-term exposure limit** is averaged over roughly 15 minutes, and it exists to guard against **acute** effects — the harm that can happen from one bad, brief, high-level exposure. Think a single spike in concentration during a specific task, rather than a slow accumulation.

The two aren't a hierarchy where one is "stricter" than the other — they're answering different questions. LTEL asks "is the average exposure across a whole shift safe over the long run?" STEL asks "even if the average is fine, was there ever a dangerous spike within any 15-minute window?" A worker's exposure could pass an LTEL check comfortably while still breaching a STEL during one intense task — and the reverse is also possible.

## Why this shows up in exam scenarios

A well-built scenario will often describe a task with a brief, intense burst of exposure (spraying, decanting, an unventilated confined task) sitting inside an otherwise unremarkable shift. If you only check the shift average against an LTEL-style limit, you'll miss exactly the risk the scenario is testing — the short, sharp spike that a STEL-style check would have caught.

## The honest limitation of exposure limits generally

Worth remembering for any question that asks you to critique OELs rather than just define them: they're generic figures, based on the best available scientific and medical knowledge at the time they were set, and they don't automatically account for combined or synergistic effects between substances used together, or for individual sensitivity. A limit that's "not exceeded" isn't automatically "safe for every individual" — it's a legal ceiling, and good practice always aims to work well under it.
