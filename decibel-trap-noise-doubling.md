# The Decibel Trap: Why Doubling Noise Doesn't Double the Number

*Meta description: Why the decibel scale is logarithmic, and why doubling sound intensity only adds about 3dB instead of doubling the figure.*
*Suggested keywords: decibel scale explained, noise doubling dB rule, NEBOSH noise calculation*
*Angle: exam-tips*

Here's a number that trips up more candidates than almost any other single fact in the noise section: if you double the intensity of a sound, the decibel reading does **not** double.

## The scale is logarithmic, not linear

Decibels measure sound on a logarithmic scale, which means equal *steps* in dB represent equal *multiples* of actual sound energy, not equal additions. Every 3dB increase represents a doubling of the sound's intensity. So an 84dB source, doubled in intensity, becomes roughly 87dB — not 168dB.

If that feels counterintuitive, that's exactly the point: it's counterintuitive, which is precisely why it's such a reliable exam trap. A distractor option that simply doubles the original number (84 → 168) will always be wrong, no matter what the actual scenario is.

## A quick way to keep the numbers straight

Every 10dB increase represents roughly a tenfold increase in sound intensity — a genuinely huge jump, even though "10dB" sounds modest written down. And a 3dB increase is enough to require noise-exposure controls to be re-assessed, since 3dB is treated as the smallest change worth acting on in most workplace noise standards. Small-sounding numbers on this scale are doing a lot of work.

## Why this matters beyond passing a maths question

This isn't just an examiner's trick question — it has a real practical consequence. If a manager assumes "we only went up 6dB, that's barely anything," they've actually authorised roughly a fourfold increase in sound energy reaching workers' ears (two 3dB doublings stacked on top of each other). Treating dB changes as small linear adjustments is exactly how noise exposure creeps upward without anyone noticing until hearing damage shows up on a health surveillance record years later.

## What actually controls noise, once you've measured it right

Once the scale itself is understood, the control hierarchy is straightforward: reduce noise at the source first (quieter machines, better maintenance), then control the pathway (enclosures, distance, damping), then limit exposure time, and only then reach for hearing protection — supported by designated zones, training and regular health surveillance (audiometry) for anyone routinely exposed. Hearing protection sits last in that list for the same reason PPE always sits last in any hierarchy: it protects only the person wearing it, correctly, every single time — which real workplaces rarely guarantee.
