# TILE in Practice: A Worked Manual-Handling Assessment

*Meta description: A worked TILE manual handling assessment example covering Task, Individual, Load and Environment, with realistic controls for each.*
*Suggested keywords: TILE manual handling assessment, manual handling risk assessment example, NEBOSH TILE explained*
*Angle: case-study*

TILE is easy to recite and surprisingly easy to apply badly, because most people jump straight to "the load is heavy" and stop there. Here's what a genuine assessment across all four letters actually looks like.

## The scenario

A warehouse worker needs to move boxed stock from a delivery pallet at floor level onto a shelf roughly four metres away, at shoulder height. It's the worker's first day. The boxes are warm from a nearby heater, the floor is worn and slippery in patches, and there's no set routine for how often this task gets repeated through the shift.

## Task

**Factor:** distance of the load from the trunk, repetitive lifting from floor level, no defined limit on how often this is repeated.
**Control:** raise the pallet to waist height so lifting doesn't start from the floor at all; if that's not immediately possible, build in job rotation or scheduled breaks so the same muscles aren't loaded all shift.

## Individual

**Factor:** this is the worker's first day — no track record of how they handle this specific task, and no manual handling training confirmed yet.
**Control:** don't assume general fitness equals competence for this task; provide manual handling training before, not after, they're put on this job, and monitor technique closely for the first few shifts.

## Load

**Factor:** the boxes are warm — an easily missed load factor that changes grip and comfort — and their size/shape may make them awkward even before weight becomes the issue.
**Control:** let the load cool where practical before handling, or provide the worker with appropriate handling aids; if the boxes are irregular or heavy, consider marking the heavier side or splitting the load into smaller units.

## Environment

**Factor:** worn, slippery flooring on the route between pallet and shelf, and a four-metre carry distance that's longer than ideal for a repetitive task.
**Control:** repair or replace the worn flooring, or reroute the task away from the affected area; shorten the carry distance where the layout allows it, and make sure lighting along the route is adequate.

## Why doing all four matters

Notice that "the load is heavy" was never actually the headline problem in this scenario — the real risk sits in the combination of a brand-new worker, a slippery floor, and an unbroken repetitive task. A risk assessment that only looked at Load would have missed the three factors most likely to actually cause an injury here. That's the entire point of forcing four separate categories: each one catches something the others structurally can't.

## A habit worth building

Before writing a single control measure, walk through all four letters and note at least one genuine observation for each — even if a category seems fine at first glance. "Environment: no issues" is a real, useful line to write, but only after you've actually checked, not skipped.
