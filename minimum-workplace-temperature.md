# The Actual Numbers Behind 'Reasonable' Workplace Temperature

*Meta description: The real minimum workplace temperature thresholds for office and physical work, and why the same rule doesn't apply to both.*
*Suggested keywords: minimum workplace temperature, thermal comfort at work, NEBOSH workplace environment*
*Angle: raw (core content)*

"Reasonable temperature" sounds like exactly the kind of vague legal phrase that could mean anything — but it actually resolves to two specific numbers, and the difference between them is worth understanding, not just memorising.

## The two thresholds

For **office-based, largely sedentary work**, the workplace should normally reach at least **16°C within the first hour** of the working day. For **physically active work**, the threshold drops to roughly **13°C**.

Why the gap? Because the human body generates a meaningful amount of its own heat through movement. Someone lifting, walking and working with their hands all shift is producing far more internal heat than someone sitting still at a desk — so the room itself needs to do less of the work to keep them comfortable. Applying the office threshold to a warehouse floor, or the warehouse threshold to a call centre, gets the underlying logic backwards.

## What actually goes into "thermal comfort"

Temperature alone doesn't tell the whole story. A genuine assessment of thermal comfort also considers **radiant heat** (heat transferred from nearby hot equipment or processes), **humidity** (how quickly sweat can actually evaporate), **air movement** (a subtle draught can make a technically-warm room feel cold), and **clothing** (protective clothing suited to a chemical hazard might be entirely wrong for a hot environment, and vice versa). Two rooms reading the same number on a thermometer can feel completely different depending on these other factors.

## Controls for heat and cold, briefly

For heat: increase distance from the source where possible, insulate hot pipework, use reflective shielding, and provide cool drinks and appropriate breaks during the hottest parts of the day. For cold: build in genuine rest cycles with warm shelter, allow extra time for tasks in cold conditions, and make sure headgear and neck protection are compatible with any other required PPE like hard hats — an easy detail to overlook until someone's actually trying to wear both at once.

## Why this belongs in the "general workplace issues" element at all

It's tempting to treat workplace temperature as too mundane to be exam-relevant compared to fire, electricity or work at height — but it's precisely because it's so easy to dismiss as "just comfort" that it's worth knowing the real numbers behind it. A workplace that's technically within a vague sense of "reasonable" but actually below 13°C for physical work has a genuine, specific, checkable problem — not a matter of opinion.
