# Ohm's Law for People Who Hate Maths: One Formula, Three Ways

*Meta description: Ohm's law explained simply with worked examples — the one calculation in the whole electricity element of the NEBOSH IGC syllabus.*
*Suggested keywords: Ohm's law explained simply, electricity calculation NEBOSH, V=IR worked example*
*Angle: exam-tips*

Here's some genuinely good news if numbers make you nervous: the electricity element of this syllabus has exactly one formula in it. Just one. Learn it properly, both ways round, and you've covered the entire numerical portion of this part of the exam.

## The formula

**Voltage (V) = Current (I) × Resistance (R)**

Voltage is what "pushes" electrons around a circuit. Current is the actual flow of those electrons, driven by the voltage. Resistance is how much the circuit itself resists that flow. Ohm's law just says these three things are locked together — change one, and the others move with it.

## Rearranging it

Because it's a simple multiplication, you can rearrange it to solve for whichever value you're missing:

- **V = I × R** — voltage equals current times resistance
- **I = V ÷ R** — current equals voltage divided by resistance
- **R = V ÷ I** — resistance equals voltage divided by current

You genuinely only need to remember one version and know how to shuffle it — but it's worth practising all three so that whichever value a question asks you to find, you're not stuck rearranging algebra under time pressure.

## A worked example

A circuit has a voltage of 230V and a resistance of 460Ω. What's the current?

Using I = V ÷ R: **230 ÷ 460 = 0.5 amps**.

The most common way to get this wrong isn't the concept — it's the arithmetic. Multiplying instead of dividing, or dividing the numbers the wrong way round, produces wildly different (and wrong) answers: try 230 × 460 and you'll get over 100,000, obviously not a sensible current for a domestic-scale circuit. If your answer looks absurd for the scenario described, that's usually a sign the formula got applied backwards, not that the scenario itself is unusual.

## Why this one calculation matters beyond the exam

Ohm's law isn't just an exam hurdle — it's the underlying reason certain electrical safety practices exist at all. It's why increasing resistance (dry skin, insulating footwear, rubber matting) genuinely reduces the current that flows through a person during contact with a live conductor, for the exact same voltage. Understanding the relationship, not just memorising the letters, makes several other parts of the electricity element click into place — resistance isn't just a number in an equation, it's the actual thing standing between a voltage and a dangerous current.
