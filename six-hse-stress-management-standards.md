# The Six HSE Stress Management Standards, With a Real Cause and Control for Each

*Meta description: The six HSE stress management standards — Demands, Control, Support, Relationships, Role, Change — with a genuine cause and control for each one.*
*Suggested keywords: HSE management standards stress, work-related stress causes NEBOSH, six stress standards explained*
*Angle: raw (core content)*

Work-related stress gets dismissed as too vague to manage properly — until you break it down into the six areas the HSE actually uses, each of which comes with recognisable causes and concrete responses. Once you see the structure, "stress" stops being a fuzzy complaint and starts being a checklist.

## Demands

**Cause:** workload too high (or too low), unreasonable patterns of working hours, or a mismatch between a worker's skills and what the job actually needs.
**Control:** set achievable targets, monitor workload actively rather than assuming it's fine, and match skills to the role rather than hoping people grow into it under pressure.

## Control

**Cause:** no say over how the work gets done, no control over breaks, no input into work patterns.
**Control:** let workers organise their own work where possible, let them have a say in their own break timing, and genuinely consult on work patterns rather than announcing them.

## Support

**Cause:** no policies that actually support workers, or support that technically exists but no one knows how to access it.
**Control:** visible, accessible support systems, sufficient training, and — often underrated — flexibility around work-life balance where the job allows it.

## Relationships

**Cause:** communication breakdowns between colleagues, poor relationships between workers and management, no system for dealing with unacceptable behaviour.
**Control:** clear, communicated disciplinary and grievance processes, a genuinely confidential route to report bad behaviour, and active work on building better working relationships rather than assuming they'll sort themselves out.

## Role

**Cause:** unclear expectations, conflicting demands between two parts of a role, being moved into a new role without training, fear of redundancy.
**Control:** a clear job description, agreed performance standards, and training that actually matches the role someone is now doing.

## Change

**Cause:** change happening with no clear reason given, no consultation before it lands, restructuring that quietly makes someone's role redundant.
**Control:** explain why the change is happening, consult properly before it's decided, and give realistic timescales rather than dropping it on people with no notice.

## Why the structure matters more than the list

The value here isn't memorising six words — it's that each standard names both a specific failure mode *and* a specific fix, which means a manager faced with a stressed team has somewhere concrete to start rather than a vague instruction to "reduce stress." A worker who says workload is fine but has zero input into their own schedule isn't struggling with Demands — they're struggling with Control, and the fix looks completely different.

## The other half of this element

Mental ill-health, workplace violence and substance misuse sit alongside stress in this part of the syllabus, and they share a pattern: recognising early warning signs (mood changes, withdrawal, deteriorating timekeeping) matters more than any single technical control, because the right response is almost always support and a genuine policy — not punishment as the first move.
