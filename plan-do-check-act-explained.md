# Plan-Do-Check-Act Isn't Just a Diagram — Here's What Goes in Each Box

*Meta description: What actually belongs in each stage of Plan-Do-Check-Act for health and safety management, with examples for each box.*
*Suggested keywords: Plan Do Check Act health and safety, ISO 45001 explained, ILO-OSH 2001 five elements*
*Angle: raw (core content)*

Almost every candidate can recite "Plan, Do, Check, Act." Far fewer can reliably say which activity belongs in which box — and that's usually what the question is actually testing.

## Plan: policy and objectives, not action

Plan is about deciding what you're aiming for and how you'll control the risks involved — writing the policy, setting objectives, assessing risks and opportunities. Nothing has actually happened yet at this stage; it's the thinking and deciding phase.

## Do: implementation

Do is where the plan becomes real: controls go in, training happens, the safe systems of work you designed on paper actually start running on the floor.

## Check: monitoring, measuring, auditing

This is the stage candidates most often misplace. Check means watching whether what you implemented is actually working — inspections, audits, measuring performance against the objectives you set in Plan. A common mistake is putting monitoring and measuring under Act instead, because "reviewing performance" sounds like something you'd do at the end. It isn't — Check is the watching; Act is what you do with what you saw.

## Act: review and improve

Act closes the loop: reviewing what Check found, and deciding what needs to change to do better next time. It feeds straight back into a revised Plan — which is the whole point of calling it a cycle rather than a straight line.

## The same idea, three frameworks

PDCA isn't NEBOSH's own invention — it's the underlying logic of both ISO 45001 (the certifiable international standard) and the ILO's own guidelines (ILO-OSH 2001), which breaks the same cycle into five elements instead of four: **Policy** (the "what"), **Organising** (the "who" — roles and communication), **Planning and implementation** (the "how" — day-to-day arrangements), **Evaluation** (looking back at performance, ISO 45001's "Check" role) and **Actions for improvement** (closing shortfalls found in evaluation).

If you see ILO-OSH 2001's five elements in a question, map them back to PDCA rather than treating them as an unrelated system: Policy = Plan, Organising + Planning and implementation = Do, Evaluation = Check, Actions for improvement = Act. Same logic, different labelling — and recognising that is often worth more marks than memorising either framework in isolation.

## Where organisations actually go wrong

In practice, most weak systems don't fail because Plan was badly written — they fail because Check never really happens, so Act has nothing real to act on. A policy that's never checked against what's actually happening on site isn't a management system; it's a document. The cycle only works if every box genuinely feeds the next one.
