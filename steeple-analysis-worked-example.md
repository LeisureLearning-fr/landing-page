# STEEPLE Analysis for Health and Safety: A Worked Example

*Meta description: A worked STEEPLE analysis example showing how each of the seven external factors shapes an organisation's health and safety priorities.*
*Suggested keywords: STEEPLE analysis health and safety, external factors health and safety management, NEBOSH STEEPLE*
*Angle: case-study*

Before an organisation can build a health and safety management system, it needs to understand the environment it's actually operating in. STEEPLE is the tool for that — seven letters, seven categories of external factor, each capable of pushing safety standards up or down depending on how the organisation responds to them.

## The seven factors

**Social** — demographics, culture, lifestyle trends, attitudes to work. **Technological** — automation, new equipment, the pace of innovation. **Economic** — interest rates, inflation, purchasing power. **Environmental** — weather, climate change, emissions. **Political** — government policy, trade stability. **Legal** — employment law, health and safety law, discrimination law. **Ethical** — reputation, business ethics, confidentiality.

## Worked example: a mid-sized frozen-food distribution company

**Social.** An ageing driver workforce and a tight labour market mean the company is recruiting less experienced staff faster than usual — which raises training and induction risk before a single truck moves.

**Technological.** New telematics systems are becoming affordable enough for a company this size — a real opportunity to monitor driver behaviour (harsh braking, speeding) rather than just reacting after an incident.

**Economic.** Rising fuel and energy costs put pressure on delivery schedules, which is exactly the kind of pressure that quietly erodes safe journey planning if no one names it explicitly.

**Environmental.** More extreme weather events mean icy or flooded routes are becoming less of an edge case and more of a routine planning factor.

**Political.** A change in road-freight regulation on driver hours directly changes what "safe" scheduling looks like — the company doesn't get to opt out of this one.

**Legal.** Updated workplace transport guidance from the relevant regulator raises the bar on pedestrian/vehicle segregation at the depot.

**Ethical.** A major retail client has started asking about safety performance as part of its own supply-chain due diligence — reputation and commercial opportunity are now directly linked to how seriously safety is taken.

## What this actually buys you

Notice that none of these seven factors are about the depot floor itself — they're all things happening *around* the organisation that change what "good" safety management needs to look like. That's the real value of STEEPLE: it stops safety planning from only ever looking inward at the last incident, and forces a look outward at what's coming next.

## A practical way to use it

You don't need all seven factors to be dramatic to make this exercise worthwhile. Pick three that are genuinely live for your own organisation right now, write one sentence on how each one is pushing risk up or down, and you'll usually surface at least one priority that wasn't on anyone's list yet.
