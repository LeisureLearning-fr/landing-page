# Means of Escape: The Eight Things a Fire Risk Assessment Actually Checks

*Meta description: The eight elements a means-of-escape assessment checks in a fire risk assessment, with the real numbers behind door ratings and travel distances.*
*Suggested keywords: means of escape fire safety, fire risk assessment checklist, NEBOSH E10 fire*
*Angle: raw (core content)*

"Means of escape" sounds like a single, obvious thing — a door, basically. In practice it's eight distinct elements, and a real assessment checks all of them, not just whether an exit exists.

## The eight elements

**Building construction.** The structure and escape routes need to resist fire long enough for everyone to get out and for emergency services to have a genuine chance of controlling the fire — not indefinitely, but for a meaningful, planned window.

**Travel distances.** Where more than one route out is available, the distance to reach safety is generally kept to around 45 metres or less — a real, checkable number, not a matter of feel.

**Stairs and passageways.** Built from fire-resisting materials, kept wide enough for genuine safe evacuation, never narrowing along the route, and never obstructed.

**Fire doors.** Rated to hold back fire and smoke for a defined period — commonly somewhere between 30 and 120 minutes depending on specification — fitted with self-closing devices and intumescent seals, and clearly marked to stay shut.

**Final exit doors.** Open outward beyond 90 degrees, clearly marked (the familiar "push bar to open"), and never locked while the building is occupied.

**Emergency lighting.** Because normal lighting is exactly the thing most likely to fail during a real fire, escape routes need dedicated lighting that shows the way out and helps people locate alarms and firefighting equipment along the route.

**Directional and exit signage.** Recognisable at a glance, placed at every junction, not just at the final door.

**Assembly points.** Far enough from the building that people aren't at risk from the fire itself, with enough space for everyone, and — a detail that's easy to miss — never positioned right next to the entrance the fire service will need to use.

## Why treating this as "just a door" misses the point

A building can have a perfectly adequate front door and still fail on means of escape, because any one of the other seven elements can break the chain — a travel distance that's too long, a fire door propped open, emergency lighting that was never tested, an assembly point that turns out to be directly in the fire engine's access route. The assessment exists precisely because none of these failures are visible from a glance at the exit sign itself.

## The other half of fire safety: prevention, not just escape

Escape planning assumes a fire has already started. The genuinely cheaper, better outcome is preventing it in the first place — keeping fuel and ignition sources apart through good housekeeping, hot-work permits, and proper electrical maintenance. Extinguishers, alarms and drills are protection and mitigation; they're not prevention, and a site that's strong on escape planning but weak on prevention is solving the wrong half of the problem.
