# Who Counts as a Lone Worker? (It's Broader Than You Think)

*Meta description: The real definition of a lone worker — it's much broader than someone physically alone in an isolated building, and it changes what the risk assessment needs to cover.*
*Suggested keywords: lone worker definition, lone working risk assessment, NEBOSH E8 general workplace issues*
*Angle: exam-tips*

Ask most people to picture a "lone worker" and they'll describe someone physically alone in an empty building at night. That's one example — but the actual definition is considerably wider, and getting it wrong means missing entire categories of risk.

## The definition, properly

Lone working covers anyone who works without close or direct supervision — which explicitly includes contractors and the self-employed, not just direct employees. It includes homeworkers. It includes people working away from a fixed base entirely. And a large chunk of what counts as "travel for work" falls under lone working too, even for someone who's ordinarily surrounded by colleagues at their normal site.

## The four situations that all count

- Working alone at a fixed base (the classic image — night security, a solo retail worker)
- Homeworking
- Working separately from other people who are technically in the same building or premises
- Working away from a fixed base entirely — visiting clients, driving between sites, delivering

That last category is easy to overlook because the person doesn't feel like a "lone worker" in the traditional sense — they might see colleagues every day. But the moment they're travelling between sites or visiting a client alone, the same risks that apply to the night-shift scenario start applying to them too.

## Why the risks aren't just "something bad happens with no one around"

Lone working risk isn't only about the dramatic scenario of an accident with no one to help. It also includes the harder-to-spot risks: no prompt access to first aid, work-related stress from isolation itself, inadequate welfare facilities when working somewhere without normal amenities, existing health conditions that could be made worse by working unsupervised, and — for certain roles — a genuinely higher risk of violence from the public.

## Controls that actually fit the definition

Because lone working covers such a wide range of situations, the controls have to flex with them: monitoring whereabouts and maintaining communication (balanced against reasonable privacy), sharing emergency contact details, following any industry-specific guidance relevant to the task, training people to handle unusual situations without panicking, periodic supervision check-ins even when someone works alone most of the time, and — for the more isolated cases — technology that can detect a fall or lack of movement and raise an alarm automatically.

## The assessment question worth asking

Before dismissing a role as "not really lone working," ask: does this person regularly do parts of their job without anyone else immediately available if something goes wrong? If the honest answer is yes — even for a contractor, even for someone who spends most of the day with colleagues but drives alone between two sites — the lone-working assessment applies to them, whether or not the job title suggests it.
