# Hazard vs Risk: The One Distinction That Decides Half Your Exam Marks

*Meta description: The difference between hazard and risk explained clearly, with the risk-matrix action bands that decide what actually has to happen next.*
*Suggested keywords: hazard vs risk difference, risk matrix bands, NEBOSH risk assessment marking*
*Angle: exam-tips*

If there's one distinction that shows up in more NEBOSH questions than any other, it's this one — and it's also the one candidates most reliably blur under exam pressure.

## The definitions, stripped down

A **hazard** is anything with the potential to cause harm. It's the source — a wet floor, an unguarded blade, a toxic chemical.

A **risk** is the likelihood that harm will actually occur, combined with how severe it would be if it did. Risk = likelihood × severity.

The trap: a question will describe a hazard and ask you to identify the risk, or vice versa, and a surprising number of candidates just restate the hazard using risk-sounding language. "The risk is the unguarded blade" isn't a risk statement — that's still describing the hazard. A genuine risk statement names the *chance and consequence*: "there's a high likelihood of a serious hand injury because the blade guard has been removed."

## Where this becomes a number

Once you've separated hazard from risk, a risk assessment usually turns both into a single figure using a matrix — typically likelihood (1-5) multiplied by severity (1-5), giving a score from 1 to 25.

But the number on its own isn't the point. What actually matters is the **band** that number falls into, because each band comes with a different required action:

- **Trivial (1-3):** no action needed, nothing to record.
- **Tolerable (4-6):** an acceptable level of risk — record it, monitor it, reduce it further only if that costs nothing.
- **Moderate (8-12):** action needed, typically within a set timescale (often three months).
- **Substantial (15-16):** must be brought down to moderate *before* the work can start at all.
- **Intolerable (20-25):** work must not proceed until the risk is reduced to an acceptable level.

This is the part of the syllabus most free quiz banks skip entirely — they'll test you on "likelihood times severity" and stop there, when the actual exam-relevant skill is knowing what each resulting number obliges you to do.

## A number worth knowing

Not every value between 1 and 25 is actually reachable on a 5×5 matrix. Since the score is always likelihood × severity with both factors between 1 and 5, numbers like 7, 11, 13, 14 and most values in the high teens to low twenties simply can't occur. If a question ever gives you an "impossible" risk score as an option, that's your signal something in the scenario's arithmetic doesn't add up.

## The five steps, briefly

Underneath all of this sits the standard five-step process: identify hazards, decide who might be harmed and how, evaluate the risk and decide on controls, record your findings, and review the assessment periodically or when something changes. Skipping straight to "what's the risk" without doing steps one and two properly is exactly how hazard and risk end up conflated in the first place.
