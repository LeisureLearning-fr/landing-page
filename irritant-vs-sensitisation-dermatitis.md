# Two Rashes That Look the Same but Aren't: Irritant vs Sensitisation Dermatitis

*Meta description: The practical difference between irritant contact dermatitis and sensitisation dermatitis — where symptoms appear, and why one comes back after the tiniest exposure.*
*Suggested keywords: irritant vs sensitisation dermatitis, occupational dermatitis explained, COSHH skin hazards*
*Angle: raw (core content)*

Two workers handle the same chemical family. Both get a rash on their hands. To the untrained eye, it looks like the same problem twice — but the underlying mechanism, and what it means for future exposure, can be completely different.

## Irritant (contact) dermatitis

This is a direct, acute reaction: the substance physically irritates the skin at the point of contact. Symptoms — cracking, flaking, redness — appear where the skin actually touched the irritant, and they stop once exposure stops. Reduce or remove the exposure, and (assuming no repeat contact) the skin recovers.

## Sensitisation dermatitis

This is an allergic response, and it plays by different rules entirely. The immune system has been triggered to overreact to the substance, and once that's happened, symptoms can appear **away from the original site of contact** — a rash on the forearm from a substance that only ever touched the hands, for instance. Worse: once sensitised, even a tiny future exposure — far smaller than what caused the original reaction — can bring the symptoms straight back.

## Why the difference actually matters

If a worker has irritant dermatitis, the practical fix is straightforward: better gloves, better hygiene, maybe a slightly different handling procedure, and the skin recovers. If a worker has sensitisation dermatitis, none of that half-measures approach works — once someone is sensitised to a specific substance, the only reliable answer is to stop their exposure to it entirely. Continuing to expose a sensitised worker "carefully" isn't a safer version of the same job; it's a different, ongoing risk that better gloves won't fix.

This is also why health surveillance for workers handling known sensitisers matters more than it might seem from the outside. Catching sensitisation early — before it's severe — gives you the option of moving someone away from the substance while the reaction is still manageable, rather than after it's become a long-term problem.

## Related, but different: respiratory sensitisation

The same underlying mechanism — an allergic overreaction that gets worse, not better, with repeat exposure — shows up in the lungs too, not just the skin. Occupational asthma triggered by sensitisers like hardwood dust or certain isocyanates follows the same pattern: once someone is sensitised, tiny future exposures can trigger a genuine asthma attack, which is why substances known to cause respiratory sensitisation get treated with real caution, not just "reasonable" ventilation.

## The practical takeaway

If a worker's skin (or breathing) problem keeps recurring despite what looks like adequate controls, or shows up somewhere the substance never actually touched, that's worth treating as a possible sensitisation case rather than assuming the existing controls just need to be "a bit better." The response to the two conditions genuinely isn't the same.
