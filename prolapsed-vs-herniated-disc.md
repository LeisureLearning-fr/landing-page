# Slipped Disc, Prolapsed Disc, Herniated Disc: What's Actually the Difference?

*Meta description: The real difference between a prolapsed disc and a herniated disc, and why "slipped disc" is a misleading term for both.*
*Suggested keywords: prolapsed vs herniated disc, slipped disc explained, manual handling back injury NEBOSH*
*Angle: raw (core content)*

"Slipped disc" is one of those phrases everyone uses and almost no one can actually define — which is exactly why it's worth five minutes to get it right, both for the exam and for talking to someone who's actually injured their back.

## Why "slipped disc" isn't technically accurate

An intervertebral disc doesn't slip anywhere — it can't, structurally. It sits fixed between two vertebrae, made of a gel-like centre wrapped in tough, layered fibrous casing (imagine a jam doughnut, sitting between two bones). "Slipped disc" is a folk term that's stuck around because it's easy to say, not because it describes what's actually happening.

## Prolapsed disc

In a prolapsed disc, the whole disc bulges outward under pressure and presses against the spinal cord or a nerve root — without the fibrous outer casing actually rupturing. Think of the jam doughnut being squeezed until it bulges out of shape, but the casing holding.

## Herniated disc

In a herniated disc, the pressure goes further: the gel-like centre actually breaches the fibrous casing and spreads out onto the spinal cord or nerve root directly. Same doughnut, but this time the casing has genuinely split and the filling has escaped.

The practical difference matters because a herniated disc — with material actually released onto the nerve — is generally the more serious injury of the two, even though both can cause significant pain depending on exactly which nerve root gets compressed.

## Why this happens at work

Discs lose flexibility with age, which is one reason older workers can be more vulnerable to this kind of injury from tasks a younger colleague might get away with. But age isn't the trigger — the immediate cause is almost always a manual handling task done with poor technique: twisting under load, lifting from an awkward position, or repeated smaller strains that eventually add up to one bad moment.

## The prevention angle, briefly

This is exactly why manual handling risk assessment exists as a distinct, structured process rather than "just be careful" — the TILE framework (Task, Individual, Load, Environment) is designed specifically to catch the twisting, awkward-posture, repetitive-strain conditions that lead to disc injuries, before someone finds out the hard way which nerve root got compressed.

## One useful habit

If you're ever describing this kind of injury to someone (or answering an exam question about it), resist the pull of "slipped disc" as a catch-all. Naming the actual mechanism — bulging casing intact, versus casing ruptured and leaking — signals you understand what's actually happening to the spine, not just the phrase people use to talk around it.
