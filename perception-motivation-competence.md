# Why Good Workers Still Make Unsafe Choices: Perception, Motivation and Competence Explained

*Meta description: Perception, motivation and competence explained with everyday examples — the three human-factors terms NEBOSH candidates most often mix up.*
*Suggested keywords: human factors health and safety, perception motivation competence NEBOSH, HSG48 human factors*
*Angle: raw (core content)*

Three terms, three very different meanings, and one exam habit of swapping them for each other. If you can keep these three straight, you'll handle a whole category of "explain why this happened" questions that trip up candidates who treat them as interchangeable.

## Perception: how you read the situation

Perception is how a person interprets the information their senses are giving them — specifically, in a safety context, how well they recognise the actual degree of risk in front of them. It's improved by training, good lighting, clear signage and removing distractions, and it's degraded by fatigue, unfamiliarity, and monotonous or repetitive work that dulls attention.

Here's the everyday version: two people walk past the exact same wet floor sign. One notices it and adjusts their stride without thinking about it. The other, distracted and tired at the end of a long shift, walks straight past it and doesn't register it at all. Same hazard, same information available — different perception.

## Motivation: the will to actually act on it

Motivation is the drive or willingness to achieve a goal — in this context, the willingness to actually work safely, not just to know how. A worker can perceive a risk perfectly clearly and still choose to cut a corner if there's no real incentive to do otherwise.

Motivation is built by involvement (being asked, not just told), recognition when someone does the right thing, decent training, and — probably the most underrated factor — visible leadership example. Discipline alone can produce compliance while someone's watching, but that's not the same as genuine motivation to work safely when no one is.

## Competence: knowing your own limits

Competence is the combination of knowledge, training or skill, and experience — including, critically, an honest awareness of the limits of your own competence. A certificate on the wall doesn't make someone competent on its own; neither does simply having done the job for years without ever being assessed against it.

The "awareness of your own limits" part is what most people forget to mention, and it's often exactly what a question is testing. A worker who knows they're not trained for a particular task and says so is demonstrating competence. A worker who assumes their general experience covers a task they've never actually been shown is demonstrating the opposite — even if nothing goes wrong that particular day.

## Why the three interact

None of these operates alone. A highly competent worker with no motivation will still cut corners. A highly motivated worker with poor perception won't notice the hazard in the first place. And someone with great perception and motivation but no real competence for the specific task is still a genuine risk — just a well-intentioned one. Good safety culture, in practice, is what you get when an organisation deliberately builds all three at once, rather than assuming one covers for the others.
