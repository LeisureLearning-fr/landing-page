# Fuse vs RCD vs Earthing: Which One Actually Saves a Life?

*Meta description: What a fuse actually protects against versus earthing and RCDs — a distinction candidates and workplaces both get wrong more often than you'd expect.*
*Suggested keywords: fuse vs RCD difference, earthing vs RCD, electrical protection devices explained*
*Angle: raw (core content)*

Ask someone what a fuse is for, and most people will say "safety" without being specific about whose. That vagueness is exactly the gap this question is designed to close.

## What a fuse actually does

A fuse is a deliberate weak link in a circuit — a thin filament designed to melt and break the circuit when current gets excessive, protecting the wiring and equipment from overheating and, ultimately, fire. That's a genuinely important job. What it is **not** designed to do is protect a person from electric shock. It reacts too slowly, and to the wrong condition (excess current in the circuit as a whole, not current passing dangerously through a person), to reliably prevent a shock.

## What earthing does instead

Earthing provides an alternative, low-resistance path to earth for fault current — so if a live conductor makes contact with an exposed metal casing, the current has a route to ground that doesn't go through whoever happens to be touching the casing at the time. It's a genuinely useful layer of protection, with one important weak point: an earth connection can become damaged or lose continuity without any obvious visible sign, silently removing the exact protection everyone assumes is still there. That's why regular inspection and testing of earthing exists as its own separate requirement, not a one-off installation check.

## What an RCD adds

A residual current device detects an imbalance between the current flowing out to a circuit and the current flowing back — the signature of current leaking somewhere it shouldn't be, such as through a person — and disconnects the supply in milliseconds. This is dramatically faster than a fuse, and it reacts to the specific condition that actually indicates a person is at risk, which is precisely the gap a fuse leaves open.

RCDs have their own real-world limitation worth knowing: they need regular testing to confirm they're still working, and some trip more often than expected ("nuisance tripping"), which can tempt frustrated workers to bypass or ignore them — turning a genuinely good safety device into one that's quietly not doing its job because people have learned to work around it.

## Putting the three together

None of the three is redundant with the others — they solve different problems. The fuse protects the wiring and the building from fire. Earthing gives fault current somewhere safe to go. The RCD reacts fast, specifically, to current that's found an unintended path — often through a person — and cuts the supply before serious harm occurs. A circuit relying on a fuse alone, with no earthing and no RCD, has real protection against fire and equipment damage, and essentially none of the fast, person-specific protection the other two devices exist to provide.

## Why the confusion persists

Part of the reason people conflate these is that they all look and sound like "safety devices" doing roughly the same job from the outside — a box in a consumer unit that cuts the power when something goes wrong. The genuinely useful exam (and workplace) skill is knowing which specific "something going wrong" each one is actually built to catch.
