# Why Extinguisher Colours Actually Matter (and What Happens If You Grab the Wrong One)

*Meta description: What each fire extinguisher colour means under BS EN3, and the real consequence of grabbing the wrong one on the wrong type of fire.*
*Suggested keywords: fire extinguisher colour codes, fire extinguisher classes explained, NEBOSH fire element*
*Angle: exam-tips*

Modern fire extinguishers are almost entirely red — at least 95% of the body has to be, under BS EN3. The colour that actually tells you what's inside is squeezed into a small band or label covering just 5-10% of the surface. That detail matters more than it sounds, because it's exactly why picking the wrong extinguisher in a panic is a genuinely easy mistake to make.

## The five types and their colours

- **Water** — red band. Class A fires (wood, paper, textiles) only. Never on electrical, metal or oil-based fires.
- **Foam** — cream band. Classes A and B (solids and flammable liquids). Still water-based underneath, so the same electrical/metal/oil restriction applies.
- **Dry powder** — blue band. The broadest single type, covering Classes A, B and C — but it leaves a residue, reduces visibility in use, and can damage sensitive electrical equipment even where it's technically permitted.
- **Carbon dioxide (CO2)** — black band. The standard choice for live electrical equipment and Class B fires, because it doesn't conduct electricity and leaves no residue. Not to be used in enclosed spaces — it displaces the oxygen in the room, which is a genuine hazard to the person using it.
- **Wet chemical** — yellow band. Classes A and F, specifically built for cooking-oil fires, where it works fast and with low risk of oil splashing back at the user.

## Why the colour genuinely matters

Because the extinguishers are otherwise almost identically sized, shaped and mostly the same colour (red), the only fast way to tell them apart under pressure — genuinely under pressure, with an actual fire in front of you — is that small coloured band. Reach for a water extinguisher on live electrical equipment because you didn't check the band, and you've just created a serious electrocution risk on top of the fire that was already there. Reach for CO2 in a small enclosed room without knowing its oxygen-displacement risk, and you've traded one hazard for another.

## Fire needs three things — remove one and it's out

Underneath the extinguisher choice sits the basic logic every fire control depends on: fuel, oxygen and an ignition source, together, at the same time. Every extinguisher type works by removing one of those three — water and foam cool the fuel below ignition temperature, CO2 and dry powder starve the fire of oxygen, wet chemical does both for oil fires specifically. Knowing *which* leg of the triangle a given extinguisher attacks is a faster way to reason about "will this work on this fire" than memorising the class letters alone.

## The practical takeaway

Extinguisher choice isn't a detail to leave to instinct in the moment — it's worth knowing, cold, before there's ever a real fire in front of you, exactly which colour band you're reaching for and why. The three seconds it takes to check is far shorter than the time it takes to deal with the consequences of getting it wrong.
