# Fixed, Interlocked, Automatic, Trip: The Machine-Guarding Hierarchy Explained

*Meta description: The FIAT hierarchy of machine guarding explained — fixed, interlocked, automatic and trip devices — with the real trade-off behind each one.*
*Suggested keywords: machine guarding hierarchy, fixed vs interlocked guard, NEBOSH work equipment guarding*
*Angle: raw (core content)*

Four types of guard, remembered by the acronym FIAT — Fixed, Interlocked, Automatic, Trip — and each one solves the same basic problem (keep a person's hands out of a danger zone) with a genuinely different trade-off, not a simple "better vs worse" ranking.

## Fixed guards: the most reliable, not the most convenient

A fixed guard is a permanent physical barrier with no moving parts — nothing to fail, nothing to misalign, nothing to forget to re-engage. That's exactly why it sits at the top of the reliability hierarchy. The trade-off: it can still be removed entirely (usually with a tool, for maintenance access), and — this is the detail worth remembering — a fixed guard isn't necessarily wired into the machine's power supply, so removing it doesn't automatically stop the machine from running. Reliability comes from the guard never needing to move, not from any built-in check that it's actually in place.

## Interlocked guards: access with a safety net

An interlocked guard cuts the machine's power the moment it's opened or removed, using an electrical, pneumatic, hydraulic or mechanical link between the guard and the machine's controls. This is the right choice when regular access to the danger zone is genuinely needed — unlike a fixed guard, which assumes access is rare. Some interlocks include an over-run delay, holding the power cut for a set time after the guard opens, to account for parts that keep moving briefly even after the machine is switched off (a spin dryer that won't open its door for 30 seconds after the cycle ends is a familiar domestic example of exactly this principle).

## Automatic guards: the least favoured, for good reason

An automatic guard physically pushes the operator's hands clear as the machine cycles, rather than simply blocking access. It's used only on slower-moving machinery, and it's generally the least preferred option in the hierarchy — because there's a real risk the operator loses their balance or overbalances as the guard moves them, which is not a risk any of the other three guard types introduce.

## Trip devices: fast reaction, not prevention

A trip device — mechanical or photo-electric, like a light curtain — cuts power the instant it's triggered, allowing genuinely clear access to the machine when it's not running while still reacting immediately if someone crosses the boundary while it is. The trade-off here is maintenance: trip devices need to be checked at the start of every single shift to confirm they're actually still working, since a fault here often isn't obvious just from looking at the machine.

## Devices that only protect the operator, not bystanders

Sitting alongside true guards are protective devices like two-hand controls and hold-to-run switches, which keep the operator's hands away from danger — but say nothing about anyone else standing nearby, who remains completely unprotected by them. A two-hand control is a genuine safety measure for the person operating the machine; it is not, on its own, a substitute for guarding the machine against everyone else in the room.

## The one-line summary worth remembering

Fixed is most reliable but least flexible; interlocked trades a small amount of reliability for genuinely needed access; automatic introduces its own new risk in exchange for convenience; trip devices react fast but need constant verification that they still work. None of the four is "the correct answer" in isolation — the right choice depends entirely on how often the danger zone genuinely needs to be accessed.
